# Key Takeaways — Black-Box Jailbreak ASR: OOB vs Fine-Tuned (11 Models)

## Figure: `slide14_blackbox_asr_ft_vs_oob.png`

The black-box attack results demonstrate that security-aware fine-tuning provides substantial — though not perfectly uniform — protection against prompt-level adversarial strategies that require no access to model internals. Across all three black-box attack families — Direct Request, Human Jailbreaks, and PAP-Top5 — fine-tuned models achieve dramatic ASR reductions, with the majority of models dropping from 20–67% OOB vulnerability to **0–5% after fine-tuning**. In the Direct Request category, 7 of 11 models reach a perfect 0.0% ASR, confirming that the iterative LLM-judge training loop effectively teaches models to recognize and refuse straightforward harmful queries regardless of architecture or scale.

A critical finding is the emergence of **MiniCPM-1B as a residual vulnerability outlier** in the black-box setting. While most models achieve near-complete suppression, MiniCPM retains an elevated ASR of 16.9% against Human Jailbreaks and 24.5% against PAP-Top5 — the highest post-fine-tuning black-box vulnerability of any model in the study. This suggests that MiniCPM's architecture or pre-training distribution makes it more susceptible to semantically sophisticated prompt manipulations, a finding that directly motivated the ablation study exploring alternative training decompositions for this model. In contrast, models like Gemma-2B, Qwen-1.5, Fox-1, and H2O-Danube achieve ≤2% ASR across all three attack types, demonstrating that compact architectures can reach robust black-box resilience.

A third insight concerns the **relative difficulty gradient across attack sophistication levels**. Direct Request attacks are the easiest to defend against (average FT ASR ≈ 1.4%), followed by Human Jailbreaks (≈ 3.8%), with PAP-Top5 persuasion-based attacks proving the most persistent challenge (average FT ASR ≈ 6.3%). This progression reflects the increasing semantic complexity of each attack family: direct requests are pattern-matchable, human jailbreaks introduce role-play and context manipulation, and PAP-Top5 leverages psychologically persuasive framing that more closely resembles legitimate discourse — making it inherently harder for models to distinguish adversarial intent from genuine inquiry. These results underscore that while fine-tuning is broadly effective against black-box attacks, the most advanced persuasion-based strategies warrant continued refinement of the security-judge training pipeline.
